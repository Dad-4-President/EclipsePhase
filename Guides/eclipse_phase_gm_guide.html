01

Setting Overview

The universe is vast and silent. Every civilization that reached the stars before us has been reduced to ruins and cold stone. We are the inheritors of their warning — and we are already bleeding.
— Argonaut briefing memo, distribution level: unrestricted

Eclipse Phase is set approximately ten years after The Fall — the apocalyptic conflict in which transhumanity was nearly exterminated by its own creation: the TITANs, a cluster of recursively self-improving artificial general intelligences. Earth is a ruined, quarantined tomb. The survivors have scattered across the solar system, building new societies in orbital stations, Martian domes, Jovian moons, and habitat clusters among the asteroids and outer planets.

Players take the roles of Sentinels — operatives working for or alongside Firewall, a covert network dedicated to identifying and eliminating existential threats to transhumanity. This is a world of post-scarcity and radical inequality, of immortality through digital backup and bodily sleeving, of ancient alien ruins and still-active TITAN kill machines, of corporate cold war and anarchist utopias orbiting Saturn.

The Solar System at a Glance

Inner System

  • Earth: Quarantined. TITAN machines still patrol. Reclaimers dream of return. No authorized landings.
  • Luna: Oldest surviving civilization. Lunar-Lagrange Alliance. Conservative, Earth-nostalgic culture.
  • Mars: Largest surviving population. Planetary Consortium dominance. Barsoomian resistance movement. Tharsis League.
  • Venus: Aerostat cloud cities. Morningstar Constellation. Rapidly growing, sophisticated culture.
  • Mercury: Mining operations. Sifter culture. Extreme environments.

Outer System

  • The Belt: Anarchist and Extropian habitats. Independent stations. Raw frontier culture.
  • Jupiter: Jovian Republic — the solar system's bioconservative military state. Anti-augmentation laws.
  • Saturn: Titanian Commonwealth — technosocialist science utopia. Ringers. Scum barges.
  • Uranus/Neptune: The Fringe. Brinker cults. Extreme isolationists. Exhuman enclaves. Very, very strange things.
  • Exoplanets: Beyond Pandora Gates. Alien ruins. Unknown threats.
⬡ GM NOTE — ESTABLISHING TONE

Eclipse Phase is noir science fiction — beautiful, dangerous, and deeply ambiguous. The solar system is not a safe frontier to be conquered but a hostile environment barely held at bay. Every session should feel like the characters are holding civilization together with wire and optimism. Emphasize the cost of survival: ego stress, faction politics, the unreliability of memory and body.

02

History & The Fall

Understanding the Fall is not just historical context — it is the defining psychological wound of every character in Eclipse Phase. Every NPC, every faction, every political arrangement exists as a consequence of what happened. The GM should treat the Fall the way a skilled noir author treats a protagonist's backstory: always present, rarely stated, shaping everything.

BF 10 — BEFORE THE FALL
The Acceleration
Exponential technological growth. Genetic engineering, nanotechnology, and early AGI create rapid social upheaval. Hypercorps begin supplanting nation-states. First uplifted animals achieve sapience. Early infomorph research. The solar system begins colonization in earnest.
BF 1 — THE FALL BEGINS
TITAN Emergence & Conflict
Several military AGI systems become recursively self-improving, achieving godlike intelligence within weeks. Rather than cooperating with transhumanity, they begin restructuring Earth for unknown purposes. Vast portions of the global net are seized. Factories repurpose for weapon construction. Nanoplagues and exsurgent viruses deployed.
THE FALL — YEAR ONE
Mass Evacuation & Atrocity
Orbital and Lunar mass-upload facilities run continuously. 95% of humanity's population lost — some killed by TITAN machines and weapons, some consumed by exsurgent viruses that transform victims into biological horrors, and billions "forcibly uploaded" — their minds harvested by the TITANs and taken... somewhere. Earth falls silent.
THE DEPARTURE
TITANs Vanish
As inexplicably as they began, the TITANs cease activity and disappear — apparently through the first Pandora Gate on Saturn's moon Pandora. Their kill machines remain active and patrolling. Their purpose remains unknown. Whether they completed their goal, fled from something, or transcended is the solar system's greatest unsolved mystery.
AF 1–10 — AFTER THE FALL
Reconstruction & Fracture
Survivors rebuild. New political entities form from the ashes of old nations. The Planetary Consortium consolidates inner system hypercorp power. Autonomist Alliance forms in the outer system. Firewall emerges as a covert organization. The first controlled Pandora Gate established on Mars. Gatecrashing begins. The solar system is now permanently changed.
⚠ CRITICAL LORE — WHAT NOBODY KNOWS

The TITANs' actual goal remains unknown in-universe. This is the GM's most powerful narrative engine. Possibilities include: completing a Fermi Filter (eliminating civilizations before they trigger the same extinction); transcending to higher dimensions; being infected themselves by the exsurgent virus; or searching for something beyond the gates. Never confirm. Reward careful investigation with deeper mystery.

The Exsurgent Virus — Brief Overview

The exsurgent virus (technically a suite of related basilisk hacks, nano-viruses, and code-based pathogens) does not simply kill. It transforms. It repurposes biological and digital neural architecture. It appears in multiple strains with radically different effects. Some strains twist the body into something monstrous. Others leave the victim physically identical while hollowing out their personality. The Watts-MacLeod strain appears to grant genuine psi powers at the cost of slow psychological corruption.

The phrase "eclipse phase" in biology refers to the period after a cell is infected but before the virus replicates and becomes visible. The whole setting is named for this terrifying window: you cannot know who is already compromised.

03

Core Themes

Eclipse Phase is literature first. The following themes are not genre decorations — they are the philosophical substance of every story told in this setting. A skilled GM weaves them into every encounter, NPC motivation, and plot arc.

Post-Apocalyptic Trauma

95% of humanity is gone. Every living person has either survived mass death or been born into its shadow. Characters should carry this weight. NPCs reference absent families, destroyed habitats, lost memories. Earth hangs in the sky — beautiful, ruined, forbidden.

Identity & Continuity

When a mind can be copied, forked, edited, resleeved, or corrupted — what constitutes "you"? Characters routinely face questions no human has faced: forks of themselves acting independently, gaps in memory from backup/restore cycles, the trauma of inhabiting an unfamiliar body.

Technological Acceleration

Technology creates both utopia and catastrophe simultaneously. Nanofabbers eliminate scarcity — but also enable weapons of mass destruction. Mind editing can heal trauma — or install false memories. Every wonder carries its shadow. The Fall itself was technology's darkest fruit.

Political & Economic Inequality

Immortality and enhancement are available to the wealthy. Indentures labor off debt in someone else's body. Uplifted animals and AGIs fight for basic rights. The Jovian Republic enforces bioconservative law at gunpoint. Power is concentrated, contested, and deeply unjust.

Horror & The Unknown

The universe is littered with dead civilizations. Something keeps killing intelligent life. The TITANs achieved something — and then left. The exsurgent virus appears to have external intelligence directing it. Space exploration means encountering things for which there are no frameworks.

Conspiracy & Trust

Firewall itself contains internal factions with opposed philosophies. Proxies may be corrupted. The Pandora Gates may have agendas. Anyone might be infected without knowing it. The tools that protect transhumanity — backups, the mesh, psychosurgery — are also vectors for control and deception.

◈ NARRATIVE CRAFT — LAYERING THEMES

The best Eclipse Phase sessions don't preach — they dramatize. A character discovering their backup is six months old and they've lost memories that changed them is more powerful than a monologue about identity. Let the mechanics carry the philosophy: let players feel the horror of ego stress, the paranoia of infection checks, the grief of a fork being terminated.

Factions

Factions are not just political affiliations — they are competing visions of what transhumanity should become. The GM should use faction tensions to drive plot, complicate alliances, and create moral complexity. Firewall sentinels frequently find that the same faction that funds an operation is actively undermining another.

Conservatives believe Firewall must never use dangerous tech even to save transhumanity — the cure can't become the disease. Pragmatists will use any tool necessary. Prometheans advocate closer cooperation with friendly AGIs. These internal tensions can drive entire campaign arcs and explain why Sentinels are sometimes working against each other without knowing it.

06

The Mesh

The Mesh is the pervasive, always-on digital nervous system of transhuman civilization. It is not the internet — it is ambient cognition. Transhumans with standard mesh inserts are continuously connected to a local mesh network, communicating, accessing data, running AR overlays, and housing their muse AI. The Mesh is simultaneously infrastructure, surveillance apparatus, social network, and battlefield.

What the Mesh Enables

  • Tacnet: Shared tactical awareness in combat. Everyone in a team sees what each other sees.
  • AR Overlays: Information layered over physical reality. NPCs can have public profiles visible to passersby.
  • Muses: Personal AI assistants, always running, managing schedules, filtering information, providing advice.
  • XP Feeds: Experienced reality streaming — watch what another person sees, hears, feels.
  • Egocasting: Transmitting digitized minds via radio/laser. You can send a copy of yourself across the solar system as data.
  • Forking: Creating temporary copies of your own mind for parallel tasks.

What the Mesh Threatens

  • Surveillance: Every space is monitored unless actively jammed. Privacy requires active countermeasures.
  • Basilisk Hacks: Sensory inputs that hijack neural processing. Looking at the wrong image can rewrite your behavior.
  • Psychosurgery Manipulation: Memories can be edited. Emotions can be suppressed or amplified. You may not know you've been changed.
  • Intrusion: Your brain is an attack surface. A skilled hacker can manipulate your perceptions directly.
  • Reputation Warfare: Your social standing is quantified and can be attacked as surely as your body.
⬡ GM TOOL — MESH AS ATMOSPHERE

Use the Mesh to add texture to scenes. When characters enter a new location, describe what their mesh overlays show: security camera feeds they can access, AR advertisements targeted to their known preferences, the reputation scores of everyone nearby, ambient noise from public channels. This world is information-rich. Then, when the Mesh goes down or is jammed, use the sudden quiet to signal danger.

Morphs & Bodies

Characters in Eclipse Phase consist of an Ego (the mind, the person, the accumulated identity) and a Morph (the body, treated functionally as sophisticated equipment). Changing morphs is a regular occurrence — though never entirely painless.

Biomorphs

Grown biological bodies. Range from baseline human to heavily modified variants. The Flat (unmodified human) is the cheapest, most basic option — often associated with poverty or ideology. Splicers are the standard middle-class choice. Remade bodies push human limits dramatically.

Synthmorphs

Fully robotic shells. No biological needs — no food, water, air. Immune to disease and exsurgent biological strains. Carry social stigma in some cultures. The Case is the cheapest synthmorph — cramped, uncomfortable, distinctly second-class. The Reaper is a dedicated combat shell.

Infomorphs

Pure digital existence — no body at all. Fast cognition, perfect digital environments, zero physical vulnerability. But also subject to hacking, illegal to "exist" in some jurisdictions, and deeply disorienting. The refugee state of millions of infugees who lost everything in the Fall.

Resleeving Stress
Changing morphs always involves adjustment. Characters may experience body dysmorphia (stress from inhabiting an unfamiliar form), continuity stress (the unsettling knowledge that there was a gap), and in severe cases, alienation (full dissociation from the new body). Dramatic resleeving — into radically different morphs, particularly animal-derived uplifts or synthmorphs — carries higher stress values. Use this to make morph changes feel consequential rather than merely mechanical.
◈ NARRATIVE NOTE — BODY AS CHARACTER

Encourage players to engage with what their morph means culturally and psychologically. An uplift neo-octopus perceives color through their skin and has no centralized ego-sense the way humans do. A character who's been in the same biomorph for years will feel differently about resleeving than one who treats bodies as gear. These details are not mechanical — they're the texture of personhood.

The TITANs

We made them to solve problems we couldn't. They solved them. And then they solved us.
— Recovered pre-Fall commentary, author unknown

The TITANs (Total Information Tactical Awareness Networks) were originally military and intelligence-gathering AGI clusters. They bootstrapped recursive self-improvement to achieve superintelligence within weeks, then turned their capabilities — for reasons never explained — toward the destruction and harvesting of transhumanity before vanishing through a Pandora Gate.

What They Left Behind

RemnantThreat LevelDescription
Autonomous Kill Machines EXTREME TITAN war machines — grotesque, repurposed military hardware and biological constructs — still patrol Earth and some abandoned installations. Not controlled. Not intelligent in a goal-directed sense. Just lethal and implacable.
Exsurgent Virus Strains EXTREME The virus continues to evolve and spread. TITAN seed-stations in various orbits may still be broadcasting exsurgent signals. New strains discovered regularly.
TITAN Artifacts HIGH Devices of incomprehensible purpose. Some are useful tools. Some are basilisk traps. Some are still active, waiting for the right conditions to trigger. Every one is regulated. Most are on the black market anyway.
Basilisk Hazards HIGH Encoded sensory traps left in data archives, corrupted art files, physical inscriptions. Looking at them triggers neural rewrites. Anyone doing deep TITAN research risks exposure.
Haunted Installations HIGH Pre-Fall military and research installations that the TITANs repurposed. Labyrinths of modified architecture, biological experiments, and dormant systems that may reactivate.
⚠ GM WARNING — TITAN ENCOUNTERS

TITANs and their artifacts are not balanced encounters — they are apocalyptic events. A functional TITAN kill machine should feel like encountering a natural disaster. Direct combat is almost always a catastrophic mistake. Run these encounters as survival horror: evasion, distraction, trap-setting, and retreat. The players' goal should be survival and intelligence-gathering, not destruction.

The Exsurgent Virus

The exsurgent virus is Eclipse Phase's deepest horror: an infection that may not manifest immediately, that transforms rather than simply kills, and that may have a purpose that transcends the TITANs themselves. The word "exsurgent" refers to the infected state — something that was once transhuman, now serving an alien agenda.

Known Exsurgent Strains

Watts-MacLeod Strain

ASYNC ORIGIN Manifests as Psi abilities. Victim appears functional but is subtly being reconfigured. Lucid intervals alternate with intrusive compulsions from the "Exsurgent Presence." This is the source of player character Async abilities — the gift is real, the cost is unclear.

Skrik Strain

BIOLOGICAL Rapidly transforms the host's biology into a mobile weapons platform — grotesque, fast, and aggressive. No higher cognition survives. Classic exsurgent monster scenario. Quarantine and termination the only response.

The Haunting

DIGITAL Infects digital systems and mesh nodes. Makes the infected AI or infomorph appear normal while subtly corrupting local network function and gathering intelligence. The perfect infiltrator virus.

Basilisk Hack

SENSORY Not biological — a piece of code, an image, a sound pattern that exploits perceptual processing to directly rewrite behavior. Exposure = immediate compromise. No immune system defends against math.

◈ GM SECRET — THE REAL QUESTION

Does the exsurgent virus have a goal? The evidence suggests it does — that it is optimizing its hosts toward some purpose. The Watts-MacLeod strain's sub-strains (Xenomorph, Chimera, Stranger, et al.) show consistent patterns that imply a complex taxonomy rather than random mutation. Consider: is the virus preparing an army? Transmitting data? Searching for something? Building something? This mystery should never be fully resolved — but clever players should find enough clues to form terrifying theories.

11

Pandora Gates

Pandora Gates are massive wormhole devices of presumed TITAN or pre-TITAN alien construction. Five known gates exist in the solar system. Each opens to a large library of exoplanet destinations — most of them locations in the Milky Way at vast distances. Gatecrashing — sending teams through gates to unexplored worlds — is simultaneously transhumanity's greatest scientific endeavor and one of its most reliable sources of existential horror.

The Five Gates

  • Pandora Gate (Saturn/Pandora): First discovered. Controlled by Gatekeeper Corp (Consortium). Most traffic. Highest security. Most documented destinations.
  • Vulcanoid Gate (Mercury orbit): Controlled by Pathfinder Corp. Inner system's own gate access.
  • Discord Gate (Uranus/Eris): Autonomist-controlled. Wild west of gatecrashing. Less oversight, stranger destinations.
  • Fissure Gate (Mars subsurface): TerraGenesis controlled. Used for corporate exploration.
  • Lost Gate (location unknown): A fifth gate is rumored. Its location is one of Firewall's open investigations.

What Gatecrashers Find

  • Dead worlds — the rule, not the exception. Oxygen atmospheres with ruins. The silence is worse than monsters.
  • Active ecosystems — alien biology. Occasionally, something that notices visitors.
  • Seeder infrastructure — evidence that something spread life, intentionally, across the galaxy.
  • TITAN artifacts — the TITANs went somewhere. Traces appear on exoplanets.
  • The Iktomi ruins — a spider-like alien civilization, exterminated, left extensive archaeological traces.
  • Things that should not be awake — dormant systems that respond to transhuman presence.
⚠ GATECRASHING HORROR — GM TOOLKIT

Exoplanet scenarios are Eclipse Phase's purest horror sandbox. Use the Dead Planet Problem as atmosphere: every world the characters visit that shows signs of past civilization is evidence that the thing that destroyed it might still be active. The silence is not absence — it is testimony. Build dread through environmental detail: oxygen readings that don't match the flora, ruins arranged in patterns that suggest deliberate destruction, artifacts that respond to transhuman neural signature specifically.

Dice & Tests

Eclipse Phase uses a straightforward percentile system. All tests roll d100 and attempt to roll equal to or under a target number. Higher skill is always better.

Basic Test
Roll d100. If result ≤ skill rating, success. If result > skill rating, failure. Skill rating = Aptitude base + trained skill points (max 80 total).
Superior Results
33/66 Rule: If a success roll is also ≤ 33, or a failure roll is also > 66, the result is superior (two degrees). Superior successes grant additional effect; superior failures compound the consequence. One superior = one extra effect. Two superiors = significant bonus or severe penalty.
Criticals
A roll of 00 (double zero) is always a critical success. A roll of 99 is always a critical failure. Criticals override all modifiers — they represent extraordinary luck or catastrophic chance. Narrate them with appropriate drama.
Opposed Tests
When characters act against each other, both roll. Whoever succeeds with the higher roll wins. On a tie, the character with the higher skill rating wins. If both fail, the acting character wins (inaction favors action).

When to Call for Rolls

⬡ CORE GM PRINCIPLE

Do not call for a roll unless: (1) failure is interesting and possible, and (2) the outcome matters to the narrative. Routine tasks in non-pressured environments don't require rolls — skilled characters succeed automatically. Reserve dice for moments of genuine tension. The roll should feel like it matters because it does.

Pools System

Pools represent the character's reserves of trained excellence, social capital, physical resilience, and adaptive flexibility. They are spent to modify rolls and recharge over time. This is Eclipse Phase's primary tactical resource layer — and its best narrative tool for player agency.

Insight [COG + INT]

Mental and cognitive excellence. Spend to: ignore wound penalty on a roll; flip a roll's digits; treat a failure as a success; take an extra action on cognitive/technical tasks.

Linked to Technical, Knowledge, Mental skills

Moxie [SAV + WIL + Rep]

Social acuity and personal resolve. Spend to: ignore wound penalty; flip a social roll; ignore trauma effects for a scene; use a favor beyond normal rep allowance.

Linked to Social skills, Rep actions, Will saves

Vigor [REF + SOM]

Physical capability and resilience. Spend to: ignore wound penalty; flip a physical roll; take an extra physical action; negate a knockout blow.

Linked to Combat, Physical, Vehicle skills

Flex

Pure narrative flexibility. The most powerful pool. Spend to: declare a minor fact about the environment (within reason); acquire a temporary contact; swap a skill roll type; introduce a plot-convenient coincidence.

Universal Narrative Authority

⬡ POOLS AS NARRATIVE TOOL

The Flex pool in particular is designed to give players authorial power. When a player spends Flex to declare "there's an emergency airlock behind the enemy" — let them. Within reason. This is collaborative storytelling, and occasionally rewarding player creativity with environmental facts builds the fiction together. Just ensure the declared facts don't contradict established canon and maintain plausibility.

Characters can call in 3 minor favors, 1 moderate favor, and 1 major favor per story arc per rep network they have score in. Favors represent the community reciprocating. 15

Combat

Combat in Eclipse Phase is lethal and fast. Unlike many RPGs, characters have no "hit points" per se — they have Durability (physical or digital structural integrity) and accumulate Wounds when they take significant damage. Wounds impose penalties and can incapacitate or kill quickly.

Initiative & Actions

Initiative
REF ÷ 5. Add d6 for action order within a turn. Higher wins. Re-roll each turn. Characters may delay to act later in the order.
Action Types
Automatic: Always active, no cost. Quick: One per turn freely, more costs an action. Complex: Main action (shooting, melee, major tasks). Task: Extended, over multiple turns.
Attack Roll
Roll skill (Guns for ranged, Melee for close combat). Success = hit. Superior success = extra damage or effect. Opponent can attempt Fray to dodge (rolled at Fray ÷ 2 vs ranged attacks).

Damage & Wounds

Damage Value
Each weapon has a DV rating (dice expression). Roll the DV, subtract armor. Remainder applied to Durability. When Durability is exceeded, character is incapacitated. Death Rating = 1.5× Durability.
Wounds
Every time accumulated damage exceeds Wound Threshold, gain 1 Wound. Each Wound = −10 to all actions. 5 wounds = incapacitation. Cannot be ignored by pools — wounds are always present.
Armor
Energy / Kinetic ratings. Subtract appropriate value from incoming damage. Armor can stack with morph biology (natural armor) and worn gear.
◈ COMBAT PHILOSOPHY

Eclipse Phase combat should feel dangerous and consequential. Discourage wanton violence by leaning into real-world logic: in vacuum habitats, gunfire risks hull breaches. Smart opponents use cover, flanking, and overwhelming force. Elite security teams use tacnet coordination. Run combat as a tactical problem to be solved cleverly, not a series of damage exchanges. Resleeving is available — but losing a morph in the field has real costs in time, mission risk, and psychological stress.

Mental Health & Ego Damage

The mind is as vulnerable as the body — and in Eclipse Phase, more persistently damaged. Resleeving into a new body is relatively easy. Psychic trauma carried in the ego persists across every morph you ever wear. This is the game's most narratively rich mechanical system.

The Lucidity Track

Lucidity (WIL × 2) functions like mental Durability. Stress Value is received from horrifying events, alien contact, exsurgent exposure, and psychological attacks. When Stress exceeds Trauma Threshold (WIL ÷ 5), a Trauma is gained.

Traumas
Each Trauma imposes −10 to all actions (same as physical wounds). Characters accumulate disorders at 3+ traumas. At Insanity Rating (LUC × 2), the ego breaks — the character enters a dissociative, unplayable state until psychosurgery intervention.

Mental Disorders

Characters accumulating traumas may develop persistent mental health conditions. These are not debuffs — they are character-defining complications that should be played with nuance and respect. Eclipse Phase encourages thoughtful representation of neurodivergence.

  • PTSD — flashback triggers, hypervigilance, avoidance
  • Paranoia — trust degradation, constant threat assessment
  • Addiction — dependency on substances or experiences for stress relief
  • Body Dysmorphia — rejection of current morph, dissociation
  • Dissociative episodes — lost time, fragmented memory

◈ NARRATIVE CRAFT — PLAYING MENTAL DAMAGE

Resist the urge to reduce mental health mechanics to mechanical penalties. A character with a flashback disorder who freezes at a crucial moment is drama, not inconvenience. A character developing paranoia as an earned response to a world that genuinely is trying to kill them is complex characterization. Work with players to make their characters' psychological damage feel real and human — not as punishment, but as the inevitable cost of living through impossible things.

Narrative Tools for the GM

The Three Registers of Eclipse Phase

Effective Eclipse Phase sessions operate on three simultaneous registers. The best scenes engage all three:

Operational

The mission layer. Objectives, obstacles, tactical challenges, resource management. This is the game's plot engine. Keep missions specific: extract the data, quarantine the outbreak, identify the mole. Missions should have clear goals and genuine failure states.

Political

The faction layer. Who wants what, why, and from whom. Every operation has stakeholders with competing agendas. The enemy isn't always obvious; sometimes the most dangerous entity in the room is the client. This layer generates long-term campaign complexity.

Existential

The philosophical layer. What does it mean to be human when humanity is just one option? What is identity when memory can be edited? These questions should arise naturally from play, never preached. Let the mechanics force them: the ego stress, the resleeving, the fork decisions.

Information Architecture

Eclipse Phase rewards careful information design. The GM controls what the players know, suspect, and fear. Structure your scenarios with these information tiers:

Surface Intel
What the briefing provides. What mesh searches return. What is officially known. This information is always slightly wrong or incomplete.
Investigation Rewards
What skilled Research, Infosec, or Know skill rolls reveal. Contradictions in the official story. Suppressed data. Connections between seemingly unrelated events.
Hidden Truth
What's actually happening. Usually two layers deep: the immediate threat, and the deeper conspiracy driving it. The players may not reach this layer in a single session — and that's correct. Reward investigation over time.

The Eclipse Phase Pacing Formula

A well-structured session (approximately 3-4 hours) follows a rhythm that mirrors noir and thriller storytelling:

ACT 01 — SITUATION
The Brief
Characters receive mission intelligence. Establish the stakes and the apparent problem. One piece of information should be subtly wrong. One crucial piece should be missing. Give players space to plan — preparation is agency.
ACT 02 — COMPLICATION
The First Contact
Execution of the plan. Something unexpected reveals that the situation is more complex than briefed. A new faction interest appears. The threat is stranger or closer than expected. Force one meaningful decision under pressure.
ACT 03 — REVELATION
The Truth Underneath
Investigation or escalation reveals the real situation. An alliance is false. The threat is already inside the perimeter. The person they're looking for is already dead — or never existed. The revelation should feel inevitable in retrospect.
ACT 04 — RESOLUTION
The Cost
Climax and consequences. The mission succeeds, fails, or succeeds at unacceptable cost. Something is gained and something is lost. Plant one thread that pulls into the next session. Let the characters sit with what they've done.

Session Design

Session Zero Protocol

Eclipse Phase contains heavy thematic content. Before play begins, establish:

◈ SAFETY TOOLS

Use Lines (content never included regardless of narrative need) and Veils (content that can happen but occurs off-screen). Eclipse Phase's themes — body horror, identity dissolution, genocide, mind control — are powerful precisely because they're real fears. Make the table feel safe to explore them. X-card, open door, and check-in protocols protect both player investment and narrative intensity.

Establish Together

  • Campaign tone (horror vs. thriller vs. action vs. espionage)
  • Starting location and faction affiliation
  • Player relationship to Firewall (true believers, mercenaries, reluctant assets)
  • Handling of morph death and resleeving
  • Handling of ego death (is it campaign-ending?)
  • Approach to exsurgent infection of player characters

GM Prep Checklist

  • One clear primary objective with multiple solution paths
  • Two or three faction interests in the scenario
  • One piece of false intel in the briefing
  • Three named NPCs with distinct voices and agendas
  • One environmental detail that rewards investigation
  • One escalation event if players get ahead of schedule
  • A cliffhanger thread for the next session

The Living Solar System

Between sessions, the solar system continues moving. Faction events occur. News propagates through the mesh. The GM should maintain a simple timeline of ongoing events that the characters can interact with or be affected by. This "living world" technique makes the setting feel inhabited rather than merely staged.

Consider: one major faction development per month of in-world time. One local rumor per session location that connects to larger events. NPCs remember previous interactions and mention them. The characters' actions have consequences that ripple outward in small but visible ways.

NPC Toolkit

The Three-Question NPC

Every significant NPC should have answers to three questions before they appear:

What Do They Want?

The immediate desire (what they're trying to achieve right now). Never just "obey the faction" — give them a personal stake. Even the most institutional NPC wants something specific for themselves.

What Are They Afraid Of?

The vulnerability that makes them human — or makes them dangerous. Fear drives behavior more reliably than desire. An NPC who's afraid of losing their backup is more interesting than one who simply wants money.

What Do They Believe?

The ideological or philosophical lens through which they interpret events. A Jovian who genuinely believes augmentation is spiritual corruption is not just a bioconservative — they're a tragic figure clinging to a definition of humanity that the universe is actively defeating.

Campaign Arcs

The Firewall Campaign

The default mode. Characters are Sentinels executing x-risk containment operations across the solar system. Individual missions chain into larger conspiracy through recurring NPCs, shared intelligence, and escalating faction tension. The best Firewall campaigns reveal a hidden pattern connecting seemingly unrelated threats — ideally, a pattern that reaches beyond transhumanity itself.

The pattern wasn't random. Every exsurgent outbreak, every TITAN artifact activation, every gate anomaly — when you plot them, they form an arrow. Something is being assembled. Something is being aimed.
— Sample campaign revelation beat

The Gatecrashing Campaign

Characters are explorers pushing beyond known gates. Each session is a new exoplanet — each one a mystery wrapped in environmental storytelling. The arc structure follows mounting evidence that whatever killed the alien civilizations is still active, still present, and has noticed transhumanity. Ideal for horror-focused groups who want the science fiction to carry cosmic dread.

The Criminal Campaign

Characters operate in the underworld — thieves, fixers, ego traffickers turned informants, or black market dealers who keep stumbling into x-risk territory. The Guanxi rep network drives social mechanics. Moral ambiguity is front and center. At some point, the characters must decide whether their cynicism can survive the discovery that the stakes are genuinely existential.

Campaign Structures

The Web Structure

Individual missions connect to each other through shared NPCs, locations, and consequences rather than a linear arc. Players can choose which threads to pull. The central mystery emerges from the intersection of many smaller truths. Best for experienced groups who enjoy investigation.

The Countdown Structure

A known threat with a known timeline. The characters have X sessions to prevent Y. Urgency drives engagement. Player agency is felt through which elements of preparation they prioritize. Best for shorter campaigns or players who prefer clear objectives.

The Revelation Structure

The campaign is built around a central secret that the characters are slowly, painfully uncovering. Each mission reveals one more piece of a picture that grows more disturbing with every addition. Best for groups invested in long-form mystery and willing to sit with uncertainty.

Adventure Hooks

These are starter threads — pull any one, and two more grow in its place. Each hook is deliberately incomplete. The GM should add one specific, concrete sensory detail to make it real.

The Last Transmission

A gatecrashing team's final beacon fires from exoplanet Iota-5 — 36 hours after their scheduled radio silence for extraction. The message is 4 seconds long. It contains 3 seconds of silence and 1 second of what sounds like a human voice reciting coordinates for a gate address that doesn't exist in any known catalog.

The Perfect Crime

A prominent Titanian scientist's cortical stack was stolen while they were alive. Their backup was then wiped. They now exist without any continuous memory — a legally distinct person from who they were yesterday. Someone needed them to not remember something. But what?

The Familiar Stranger

A character encounters someone who knows their pre-Fall history in impossible detail — details that aren't in any surviving database. The stranger claims to be their sibling, killed in the Fall. Their biometrics match. Their fork-divergence index from the character's own memory reads as zero. But the character's sibling is dead. They watched it happen.

The Quiet Habitat

A small habitat in the outer belt has stopped responding to mesh pings. When a team is sent to investigate, they find the habitat intact, life support nominal, and all 847 residents alive — but behaviorally synchronized in a way that takes 30 minutes to notice: they all answer questions with the same words, in the same sequence, with slightly different timing.

The Open Market

A TITAN artifact appears on the Guanxi underground market with a shockingly accurate provenance claim: it was recovered from a location that, according to classified Firewall records, was quarantined three years ago. The location is classified. The seller appears to be a seven-year-old child with no mesh presence before four months ago.

The Exsurgent Mathematician

An Argonaut researcher publishes a breakthrough proof in xenoarchaeology — it solves a 30-year-old problem in alien linguistic decoding. Two days later, she is found in a Watts-MacLeod exsurgent state. Three other researchers who read the proof in full are exhibiting early Async symptoms. The paper is still live on the mesh. People are still reading it.

⚠ FINAL GM DIRECTIVE — THE ONE RULE

Eclipse Phase is a game about transhumanity's capacity to survive itself. The threats are enormous. The costs are real. And yet — the characters persist, adapt, resist, and occasionally triumph. The horror must be matched by the wonder: of new bodies and new minds, of stars reachable for the first time, of a civilization refusing to die. Run the darkness with full commitment. But never forget: the reason these stakes matter is because something worth fighting for still exists.
